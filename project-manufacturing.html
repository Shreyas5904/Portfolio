Flexible Stall Strip Testing & Development

Aug 2024 - Present Duke Aeroelasticity Lab

Overview

Investigation of flexible stall strips as passive flow control devices for General Aviation (GA) aircraft. This research explores how stall strip geometry, placement, and material properties affect stall onset, drag characteristics, and overall handling qualities of a NACA 0012 airfoil. The core research question examines how stall strips can enable smoother stall behavior and lower separation speeds while minimizing aerodynamic penalties.

The project combines systematic wind tunnel testing, computational design, and manufacturing to develop and validate flexible stall strip designs. These strips serve as boundary-layer-tripping elements that improve flow stability and delay stall conditions across various flight regimes—directly addressing practical GA aircraft performance and safety requirements.

Research Objectives & Core Questions

This research addresses critical aerodynamic design challenges for improving aircraft handling:

  • How do stall strip geometry, size, and spanwise placement affect stall onset and onset smoothness?
  • What is the drag penalty imposed by stall strips pre-stall, and how does it vary with strip configuration?
  • Can flexible (TPU) strips provide superior stall control compared to rigid alternatives?
  • How does root-biased strip placement optimize both stall behavior and aerodynamic efficiency?
  • What is the relationship between Reynolds number and stall strip effectiveness at low speeds?
  • How can experimental findings translate to real-world GA aircraft design improvements?

Experimental Approach

Test Airfoil & Configuration

  • NACA 0012 symmetric airfoil (clean baseline, predictable stall characteristics)
  • Removable stall strip interface for quick configuration changes
  • Adjustable angle of attack mechanism (steel rod with bolt-and-nut mounting)
  • Precision force measurement capability (≥100 Hz sampling rate)

Stall Strip Configurations

  • Geometry: Triangular strips (baseline, aircraft-relevant design)
  • Heights: 1 mm, 2 mm, 3 mm (scaled to chord)
  • Spanwise Lengths: Short (root-local), medium, and extended options
  • Materials: Rigid (PLA/ABS) vs. flexible (TPU) for comparative analysis
  • Placement: Root-biased positioning near leading edge

Testing Methods

  • Method 1 (Primary): Fixed Reynolds number with angle of attack sweep
  • Captures stall AoA, hysteresis behavior, and drag characteristics
  • Method 2 (Secondary): Fixed angle of attack with Reynolds number variation
  • Identifies separation onset by velocity and scale sensitivity

Instrumentation & Measurement

  • Three-axis force balance (lift, drag, moment measurement)
  • Tuft visualization to observe separation onset and oscillation patterns
  • Mean and RMS force data acquisition at ≥100 Hz
  • Hysteresis mapping during ramp-up and ramp-down cycles
  • Documented separation patterns via photography and video

Design & Manufacturing Approach

CAD Design & Optimization

  • Parametric NACA 0012 model with removable strip interface
  • Multiple strip heights and spanwise length variations
  • Design optimization for structural integrity and aerodynamic consistency
  • Integration of mounting points for angle of attack adjustment

Fabrication & Material Selection

  • High-precision manufacturing using additive techniques for prototyping
  • Rigid strip fabrication (PLA/ABS) for baseline comparison
  • Flexible strip integration using TPU material for novel control capabilities
  • Surface finish optimization for aerodynamic consistency and repeatability

Assembly & Calibration

  • Modular assembly design enabling quick strip configuration changes
  • Precision angle of attack adjustment mechanism with reference calibration
  • Force balance zeroing and tare procedures
  • Dry-run verification of data acquisition systems

Wind Tunnel Testing Protocol

Comprehensive testing follows a systematic, phased approach to ensure data quality and consistency:

Phase 1: Baseline Testing (Clean Airfoil)

  • Establish reference C_L(α) and C_D(α) curves for clean NACA 0012
  • Identify natural stall AoA and hysteresis characteristics
  • Quantify RMS lift oscillations near stall and natural noise floor
  • Repeated runs to ensure reproducibility and confidence

Phase 2: Systematic Configuration Testing

  • One variable tested at a time (size, span extent, material)
  • Identical angle of attack protocol for all configurations
  • Record mean and RMS forces across full angle of attack range
  • Tuft visualization documenting separation patterns and dynamics
  • Repeat critical test cases near stall for enhanced confidence

Phase 3: Data Extraction & Analysis

  • Calculate C_L(α) and C_D(α) for each configuration
  • Quantify stall onset metrics (AoA, smoothness, hysteresis)
  • Evaluate drag penalty pre-stall compared to baseline
  • Compare size effects, spanwise extent effects, and rigid vs. flexible performance

Key Findings & Results

  • Established baseline aerodynamic performance for clean NACA 0012 airfoil
  • Quantified effects of stall strip height on stall onset and drag characteristics
  • Demonstrated spanwise placement effects on flow control effectiveness
  • Compared rigid vs. flexible strip performance across angle of attack range
  • Identified optimal strip configurations balancing stall control with minimal drag penalty
  • Generated comprehensive dataset supporting design recommendations for GA applications

Applications & Real-World Impact

Stall strips offer significant practical benefits for General Aviation aircraft:

  • Improved aircraft safety through extended stall margin and more predictable stall behavior
  • Enhanced handling characteristics in low-speed flight regimes critical to GA operations
  • Passive flow control requiring no active systems, power, or pilot input
  • Minimal structural modifications with cost-effective implementation
  • Applicability across fixed-wing aircraft, UAVs, and rotorcraft platforms
  • Potential for adaptive control in variable flight conditions using flexible materials
